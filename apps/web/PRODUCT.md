# Product

## Register

product

## Users

SCHNGN is for non-EU travelers who need to understand the Schengen 90/180-day rule before and during travel. The core users are frequent travelers, second-home owners, remote workers, family planners, and people coordinating multi-country trips where a mistake can create real border anxiety.

They usually arrive with one urgent question: can this planned trip fit? They are often on a phone, reviewing dates from bookings, family calendars, or old travel records. They need a clear answer first, then enough proof to trust it.

## Product Purpose

SCHNGN is a privacy-first Schengen 90/180-day tracker and trip planner. It tells a traveler whether a planned Schengen trip is safe, explains why in plain English, and keeps trip data on the device.

Success means the user can answer these questions quickly:

- Does this trip fit?
- How many safe buffer days remain?
- What is the must-exit-by date?
- Which trips are counted in the rolling 180-day window?
- When do days come back?
- If the plan is unsafe, what must change?

The product is not a legal-advice workflow. It is a calculation instrument with clear disclaimers, inspectable evidence, and local-only storage.

## Brand Personality

Precise, calm, private, and evidence-led.

SCHNGN should feel like a serious calculation instrument softened for anxious travelers. It should have the credibility of a border-ready ledger, the reassurance of a public-service tool, and the usability of a mobile consumer app.

Voice principles:

- Say the answer plainly: "Italy fits", "4 days over limit", "Must exit by Oct 13".
- Use proof language, not legal theatre.
- Treat privacy as a product status, not a marketing slogan.
- Be calm when the answer is safe and decisive when it is unsafe.

## Anti-references

SCHNGN must not look or sound like:

- Generic SaaS dashboard cards that bury the answer.
- AI-purple gradient travel-tech.
- Airline, suitcase, globe, passport-stamp, map-pin, or a literal official-looking EU flag/emblem clone. Abstract EU-stars-inspired identity is allowed when it reads as SCHNGN, not as an EU institution.
- Cybersecurity control-room dark mode.
- Legal-service intimidation with shields, scales, and pseudo-compliance language.
- App Store gloss with huge rounded cards, decorative shadows, and fake delight.
- Government beige sludge that feels official but not usable.
- Circular gauge as the main explanation. The rule is a rolling window, not a fixed bucket.
- Raw tables as the default mobile explanation.

Avoid these words in product copy unless there is a specific technical context: payload, object, schema, draft, compliance object, protected.

## Design Principles

1. **Answer first, proof second.** The primary screen must answer "can I go?" in under five seconds. The calculation proof should be one gesture away, never hidden behind mystery.

2. **Make the rolling window visible.** The 180-day period is the product's central mental model. Use a timeline/window/lens, not a generic progress gauge.

3. **Show counted evidence.** Every verdict should expose which trips counted, which days were used, and what changes when the plan moves.

4. **Separate booked from what-if.** Booked trips and simulations are different user commitments. The UI must make that distinction obvious without suggesting booked trips are legally protected.

5. **Local-first is part of trust.** Trip data stays on the device. Analytics and waitlist flows must not receive trip dates, full travel histories, or calculated personal timelines.

6. **Risk states deserve the same craft as safe states.** Unsafe plans should be specific, fixable, and non-panicky: how many days over, why, and the first correction.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

Target WCAG 2.2 AA or better for the web app. Body text, muted text, placeholders, form labels, chips, and status text must pass contrast requirements against their actual backgrounds.

Design must not rely on color alone. Safe, booked, what-if, and risk states need distinct labels, shapes, icons, and/or patterns.

The app must support small mobile screens, keyboard navigation, screen readers, reduced motion, and clear focus states. Motion should communicate state changes only and should be disabled or simplified under `prefers-reduced-motion`.

Copy must remain plain English. The product should be usable by travelers who are worried, distracted, or not native English speakers. Explanations should avoid legal advice, jargon, and unnecessary precision beyond the calculation itself.
